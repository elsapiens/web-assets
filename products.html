1.1
Items (Standalone)

Items are the basic unit. Each represents a single product, service, or utility.

Goods (Products)

Physical items with inventory tracking. Has stock, can be stored, valued using FIFO/Weighted Avg.

E.g., Laptop, T-Shirt, Coffee

Services

Non-physical items, no inventory. Delivered, not stored. Time or task based.

E.g., Installation, Consulting, Repair

Utility

Consumable resources billed by usage. Metered, no physical stock. Usage-based billing.

E.g., Electricity, Water, Bandwidth

Unit of Measure (UOM) System

Items can be bought, sold, and tracked in different units. The UOM system manages these conversions.

Base UOM

The default unit for inventory tracking. All stock quantities stored in this unit.

e.g., Piece, Kilogram, Liter

Alternate UOMs

Other units the item can be bought or sold in.

e.g., Box, Case, Dozen

Conversion Factors

How alternate units relate to the base unit.

e.g., 1 Box = 12 Pieces

UOM Conversion Example

UOMTypeConversion
Piece Base 1
Box Alternate 1 Box = 12 Pieces
Case Alternate 1 Case = 4 Boxes = 48 Pieces

Use Cases

Purchasing

Buy in Cases from supplier (bulk pricing)

B2B Sales

Sell in Boxes to retailers (wholesale)

B2C Sales

Sell in Pieces to consumers (retail)

Inventory

Always tracks in base UOM (Pieces)

How Conversions Work

When you sell 2 Boxes, system automatically deducts 24 Pieces (2 x 12) from inventory. When you receive 1 Case, system adds 48 Pieces to stock. All reports and stock levels show base UOM for consistency.

Why Dimensions Matter

Shipping Carriers

Carriers calculate cost by dimensional weight (DIM weight). Large but light items may cost more to ship than their actual weight suggests.

Warehouse Slotting

Determines where items are stored - shelf size, bin allocation, pallet configuration. Proper slotting improves picking efficiency.

Package Optimization

System can suggest optimal box sizes for orders. Reduces shipping costs and packaging waste.

DIM Factor Explained

DIM Factor (Dimensional Factor) is a number used by shipping carriers to calculate dimensional weight (volumetric weight).

DIM Weight = (Length Width Height) DIM Factor

Common DIM Factors

Carrier/ContextDIM FactorUnit
Domestic (US)139cubic inches
International166cubic inches
Metric5000cubic cm

Example Calculation

A box measuring 20" 15" 10" with actual weight of 5 lbs:

  • Volume = 20 15 10 = 3,000 cubic inches
  • DIM Weight = 3,000 139 = 21.6 lbs
  • Carrier charges for 21.6 lbs (not 5 lbs) because DIM weight is higher

Why DIM Factor Exists

Carriers have limited truck/plane space. A large, light package takes up room that could hold heavier, denser items. DIM pricing ensures they recover revenue from bulky shipments.

Remember: Lower DIM factor = higher shipping cost. A DIM factor of 139 results in higher DIM weight than 166 for the same box size.

Why Commodity Library Matters

Different products need different data. A Green Cardamom needs Moisture %, Grade Distribution, and Harvest Year. A Laptop needs RAM, Storage, and Processor specs. Without a commodity library, every business would manually configure these fields - leading to inconsistency, missing data, and wasted effort. Financial Parameters

Items are commercial definitions. The transaction context determines how they're treated financially. This is event-driven accounting.

The Core Principle

The same item can be Inventory, Expense, or Fixed Asset - depending on how it's used. The item doesn't decide accounting; transactions do.

Inventory

Purchased for resale

Expense

Consumed in operations

Fixed Asset

Long-term internal use

Event-Driven Architecture: How It Works

Items hold parameters. Events read those parameters and create journal entries. The journal is the source of truth.

ITEM
Holds parameters
valuation method, HSN, etc.
EVENT
Triggers processing
purchase, sale, transfer
JOURNAL ENTRY
Source of truth
Dr/Cr accounts

Purchase Event

  1. PO received triggers purchase event
  2. System reads item's valuation method
  3. System reads item's HSN determines tax
  4. Cost layers updated (FIFO) or average recalculated
  5. Journal entry created:
    Dr Inventory | Cr Accounts Payable

Sale Event

  1. Invoice created triggers sale event
  2. System reads item's valuation method
  3. COGS calculated (FIFO: oldest layer, or avg cost)
  4. Store's price list applied selling price
  5. Journal entries created:
    Dr COGS | Cr Inventory
    Dr Receivable | Cr Revenue
Key Insight Items don't "do" accounting. Items are data. Events read item data, apply policies, and create journal entries. The sections below define the parameters that events consume.

Tiered Pricing Explained

Tiered pricing allows you to set different margins based on quantity purchased. Higher quantities get lower margins, incentivizing bulk purchases while protecting margins on small orders.

Quantity RangeMargin %Unit Price (on $100 cost)Rationale
1 - 10 units 30% $130.00 Small orders - full margin to cover handling
11 - 50 units 20% $120.00 Medium orders - reduced margin for volume
51 - 100 units 15% $115.00 Large orders - bulk discount pricing
101+ units 10% $110.00 Wholesale - minimum margin for major buyers

When to Use Tiered Pricing

  • B2B sales with varying order sizes
  • Wholesale vs retail customers
  • Encouraging larger orders
  • Products with high per-order handling costs

How Tiers Are Applied

  • System checks quantity in cart/order
  • Applies the matching tier's margin
  • All units priced at that tier (not graduated)
  • Recalculates if quantity changes
Example: Customer Orders 25 Units

Cost per unit: $100 | Tier matched: 11-50 units (20% margin)
Unit price: $100 + 20% = $120
Order total: 25 $120 = $3,000 (instead of $3,250 at 30% margin - customer saves $250)

Tiered Pricing + Discounts: How They Stack

Tiered pricing and discounts work together, not against each other. They are applied sequentially:

Tiered Pricing

  • Part of Price List configuration
  • Determines the selling price based on quantity
  • Applied at business level

Discounts & Offers

  • Applied ON TOP of price list price
  • Reduces from the tiered price
  • Applied at store level

4.4
Inventory Valuation

Determines how you calculate the cost of items in stock. Each item has ONE valuation method.

FIFO (First In, First Out)

Oldest inventory sold first. COGS reflects older prices.

Jan: 10 @ $100
Feb: 10 @ $120
Sell 5: 5 $100 = $500
Best for: Perishables, dated inventory

Weighted Average

All units valued at average cost. Each purchase updates average.

Jan: 10 @ $100
Feb: 10 @ $120
Avg: $110
Sell 5: 5 $110 = $550
Best for: Commodities, homogeneous items
One Method Per Item Cannot use FIFO for one store and Weighted Average for another. Must be consistent across all locations for accurate accounting.

Best Practice

Set reorder points based on lead time and average daily sales. Formula: Reorder Point = (Lead Time in Days Daily Sales) + Safety Stock. Review and adjust quarterly based on seasonal demand patterns.

Fixed Assets & Depreciation

When items are purchased/produced for long-term internal use (not resale), they become Fixed Assets that lose value over time through depreciation.

Asset Categories

Define default depreciation rules for groups of similar assets:

CategoryUseful LifeSalvageMethodFrequency
Office Furniture7 years10%Straight LineMonthly
Computer Equipment3 years$0Straight LineMonthly
Vehicles5 years15%Declining BalanceMonthly
Machinery10 years5%Straight LineMonthly
Buildings30 years20%Straight LineAnnually

Depreciation Methods

Straight Line

Same amount every year. Simple and predictable.

(Cost - Salvage) / Useful Life
Example: $10,000, $1,000 salvage, 5 years
($10,000 - $1,000) / 5 = $1,800/year

Declining Balance

Higher depreciation early, less later. Based on remaining book value.

Book Value Rate (Rate = 2 / Life)
Example: $10,000, 5 years, 40% rate
Y1: $4,000 | Y2: $2,400 | Y3: $1,440...
Why Categories Matter: Without categories, users manually enter parameters for every asset. Categories enforce consistency while allowing overrides when needed.

ScenarioRecommendationRationale

Customer can pay cash upon delivery

  • Builds trust with new customers
  • Essential in low digital payment markets

COD Disabled (Prepaid Only)

Customer must pay before shipping

  • Reduces fraud and refusal rates
  • Improves cash flow

Best Practice
